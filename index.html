Subtheme: Exploring corporate owners and evictions

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Is the disparity in eviction filings between live-in/live-nearby landlords compared to absentee/corporate landlords statistically significant? Is it concentrated/driven by certain neighborhoods, socioeconomic demographics, or other factors? How has COVID impacted this disparity?
    • Motivation: this disparity is clearly a problem, but it is likely so widespread it would be difficult to prioritize initiatives to tackle the issue without some measure of severity
  2. Where has corporate ownership overtime experienced the biggest change? Does normalizing either for land area or some measure of wealth change the which neighborhoods are the biggest hot spots? What does this imply about future trends in corporate ownership if nothing is done? Are there any guardrails that could stop or reverse any trends today?
    • Motivation: Just want to understand if there is saturation in corporate ownership or if we are still in the middle of a long wave. This can provide some context on the scale of the issue was well as where we need to focus without simply looking at totals which is why the normalization piece feels important to me.
  3. Which demographics were impacted COVID evictions more than others? Did corporate ownership accelerate or slow during COVID?
    • Motivation: COVID obviously had an enormous impact on housing and the financial sector and I am curious to know if corporate owners got more aggressive either in evictions or in buying up properties as a result.

Discoveries & Insights

After actually digging into the data, I realized a few major things. Firstly, there is no eviction data. There is 2020 census data, corporate ownership data over time (2004-2024), and owner occupancy data over time (2004-2024). The demographic data being only a snapshot from 2020 meant I had to be careful about when I used sum vs average vs filtering for a specific year. I learned this the hard way when the population of the metro Boston area was showing up over 13M initially. The description of the dataset and sub-theme made me think there was eviction data with fields for live in/live nearby landlords vs corporate landlords, but unfortunately that is not the case. I was able to pivot my analysis and visualization to focus understanding where different demographics of people live and what the corresponding trends in corporate ownership and owner occupancy is in those areas. Given the large number of neighborhoods also I found sizing and readability to an issue throughout the visualization process and something I'd like to improve on in the future.

Summary

Ultimately, I was unable to answer most of the questions that initially got me interested in the data set around evictions, but there was still some extremely interesting insights to be drawn from the data. The role of neighborhoods, race, and education were the primary ones I focused on. There were multiple questions and next steps that came to mind as I was conducting the exploratory portion of the analysis and thoughts about predicting corporate ownership rates are still flying through my head now. I would also love to start to look for third party data on evictions in order to go back and answer some of my initial questions. There is a lot of concern that can stem from the rapid increase in corporate ownership rates over the last 20 years. I focused on neighborhood breakdown, but other demographics could also be used though the limitation of the census data being from 2020 made me hesitate to plot corporate ownership rates for say nursing homes over time when the total population in nursing homes is a flat value from 2020. There are plenty of further questions to be asked and answer on this data set and other related data sets.
